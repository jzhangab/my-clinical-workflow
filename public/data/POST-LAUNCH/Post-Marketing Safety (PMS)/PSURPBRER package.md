**1. Executive Summary**

Cervimab (CERVILUX®) is a humanized IgG4 anti-PD-1 monoclonal antibody
approved for the treatment of adult patients with dMMR/MSI-H metastatic
colorectal cancer (mCRC) following prior fluoropyrimidine-,
oxaliplatin-, and irinotecan-based chemotherapy. This Periodic
Benefit-Risk Evaluation Report (PBRER) covers the first six months of
post-marketing experience, spanning April 1 through September 30, 2025.

During this reporting period, an estimated 2,100 patients in the United
States received at least one dose of Cervimab in the commercial setting.
The cumulative post-marketing exposure represents approximately 312
patient-years. No new safety signals have been identified that were not
previously characterized in the Reference Safety Information (RSI). The
overall benefit-risk profile of Cervimab for the approved indication
remains positive and unchanged.

The most commonly reported immune-mediated adverse events (imAEs) ---
including immune-mediated colitis (7.2%), pneumonitis (3.8%), and
hepatitis (2.6%) --- are consistent with the established class-effect
safety profile of PD-1 inhibitors and are reflected in the current
Prescribing Information (USPI). No new or unexpected patterns of serious
adverse events were identified in spontaneous post-marketing reports.

The PBRER concludes that no changes to the USPI, Risk Evaluation and
Mitigation Strategy (REMS), or Risk Management Plan (RMP) are warranted
at this time. Ongoing pharmacovigilance and the long-term safety
registry (CERVIMAB-LTS-01) continue to operate as planned.

**9. Integrated Benefit-Risk Assessment**

The benefit-risk balance for Cervimab in the approved indication of
dMMR/MSI-H mCRC remains clearly favorable. The unmet medical need in
this population---who have exhausted standard-of-care options---is high,
with historical median OS of approximately 6 months on BSC. Cervimab\'s
durable response rates and OS benefit, combined with a manageable
immune-mediated safety profile, support continued use in the approved
indication.

Identified risks (immune-mediated adverse events, infusion-related
reactions) are class-effects of PD-1 inhibitors, are addressed in the
USPI with comprehensive monitoring and management guidance, and are
well-understood by treating oncologists. No new safety concerns have
emerged during the first six post-marketing months.

**D. Adverse Event Narrative**

A 67-year-old male with dMMR/MSI-H mCRC initiated Cervimab 200 mg IV on
March 25, 2025 (Cycle 1). Seven days after Cycle 2 administration (April
22, 2025), the patient presented to the emergency department with chest
pain, dyspnea, and fatigue. Troponin-I was markedly elevated (18.4
ng/mL; ULN 0.04 ng/mL). ECG showed new diffuse ST-segment changes.
Echocardiogram demonstrated reduced LVEF (30%, baseline unknown but
prior echocardiogram 3 months prior was normal).

Cardiac MRI confirmed diffuse myocardial inflammation consistent with
immune-mediated myocarditis. CMR showed late gadolinium enhancement in a
non-ischemic distribution involving \>50% of the myocardium.
Endomyocardial biopsy demonstrated lymphocytic infiltrate with CD8+
T-cell predominance on immunohistochemistry, consistent with
immune-checkpoint inhibitor-associated myocarditis.

Cervimab was permanently discontinued. High-dose methylprednisolone
1,000 mg IV was initiated on Day 1 of hospitalization. Despite
aggressive immunosuppression and hemodynamic support, the patient
developed complete heart block requiring temporary pacemaker insertion.
On Day 8 of hospitalization (April 30, 2025), the patient developed
ventricular fibrillation refractory to defibrillation and expired. Cause
of death: fatal arrhythmia secondary to immune-mediated myocarditis.

Causality Assessment: Related (Probable). This case is consistent with
the known rare but serious risk of immune-mediated myocarditis
associated with PD-1 inhibitors. This event is described in the Cervimab
USPI under Warnings and Precautions (Section 5.1 --- Immune-Mediated
Adverse Reactions) and carries a BLACK BOX WARNING. Reporting
obligation: 15-day expedited report to FDA. Case submitted April 28,
2025 (Day 0 = date of receipt of complete information).

**C. Narrative**

A 54-year-old female receiving Cervimab for dMMR mCRC presented on Day
22 after Cycle 3 with a 5-day history of watery diarrhea (10+
stools/day), lower abdominal cramping, and blood in stool. She was
hospitalized for IV fluid resuscitation and evaluation. Colonoscopy
revealed diffuse ulcerative colitis affecting the entire colon with loss
of haustral markings (Mayo Score 3). Histopathology showed diffuse
cryptitis with intraepithelial lymphocytes consistent with
immune-mediated colitis (Grade 3 per CTCAE v5.0). Infectious causes (C.
difficile, CMV, bacterial culture) were negative.

Cervimab was withheld. IV methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg/day was initiated.
The patient did not improve after 5 days, and infliximab 5 mg/kg IV was
administered (Day 6 of hospitalization). Symptoms improved substantially
within 48 hours of infliximab. Patient was discharged on oral prednisone
taper (Day 11). Colonoscopy performed 6 weeks post-discharge showed
resolution of mucosal inflammation. Cervimab was permanently
discontinued per protocol. Patient subsequently enrolled in long-term
safety registry.

Causality: Related (Definite). Outcome: Recovered with sequelae
(permanent Cervimab discontinuation). Seriousness: Hospitalization,
medically significant. MedDRA PT: Colitis (10009888), Grade 3. Listed in
USPI Section 5.2.

**C. Narrative**

A 48-year-old male receiving his first Cervimab infusion developed
flushing, urticaria, rigors, and oxygen desaturation to 88% on room air
approximately 15 minutes into infusion. Infusion was immediately
stopped. The patient received IV diphenhydramine 50 mg, IV
methylprednisolone 125 mg, and supplemental oxygen 4L/NC. Vital signs
stabilized within 20 minutes (O2 sat 97% on RA). Patient was monitored
for 4 hours and discharged in stable condition.

Causality: Related (Definite). CTCAE Grade 2 IRR. Outcome: Recovered
completely. Cervimab was subsequently re-introduced at Cycle 2 with
premedication (diphenhydramine, acetaminophen, dexamethasone) per USPI
guidance without recurrence. This case was reported per the DHCP letter
guidance distributed July 2025 and is consistent with the labeled
infusion-related reaction profile. MedDRA PT: Infusion related reaction
(10051792). Listed; non-expedited periodic reporting.

**IV.B. Additional Risk Minimization --- REMS**

Cervimab is subject to a Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy (REMS)
program --- CERVILUX REMS --- as required under 21 CFR 505-1. The REMS
is described in detail in Document 5 (REMS Assessment Report). Key REMS
elements include: (1) Healthcare provider enrollment and training
certification; (2) Healthcare facility enrollment; (3) patient
enrollment with signed Informed Acknowledgment form; and (4) a Patient
Safety Registry.

Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy (REMS) for cervimab (CERVILUX®) since

of immune-mediated toxicities --- particularly myocarditis, pneumonitis,
and colitis --- in patients receiving Cervimab for dMMR/MSI-H mCRC.

Assessment conclusions: The CERVILUX REMS program is functioning as
designed. Healthcare provider and facility enrollment rates, patient
informed acknowledgment completion, and imAE recognition/management
outcomes demonstrate that the REMS is meeting its goals. Prescriber
compliance rates exceed the pre-specified 85% threshold. Two REMS
program elements are recommended for modification based on first-year
learnings, as described in Section 6.
