**INTRODUCTION: WHAT IS THIS DOCUMENT?**

You are being invited to take part in a research study. Before you
decide whether to participate, it is important for you to understand why
the research is being done and what it will involve. Please take time to
read the following information carefully. Talk to others --- your
family, your doctor, or your friends --- if you wish.

This form tells you about a clinical study testing an investigational
(not yet approved for general use) drug called Cervimab in people who
have colorectal cancer that has spread (metastatic) and has a specific
characteristic called dMMR or MSI-H (explained below). You do not have
to agree to take part. Your decision will not affect your regular
medical care in any way.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------
  **Taking part in this study is completely voluntary. You may
  withdraw at any time without penalty or loss of benefits to which
  you are otherwise entitled.**

  -----------------------------------------------------------------

**KEY INFORMATION SUMMARY**

Before you read the full document, here are the most important things
you should know:

- This study tests whether a new drug called Cervimab works better than
  the current standard chemotherapy for your type of cancer.

- You will be assigned by chance (like flipping a coin) to receive
  either Cervimab or standard chemotherapy. You have an equal 50/50
  chance of receiving either.

- Neither you nor your doctor will know which treatment you receive
  (this is called double-blind).

- Your treatment will be given by IV (intravenous infusion). You will
  visit the clinic regularly for treatment and tests.

- Taking part is voluntary. You can stop at any time.

- All research involves some risks. The risks and benefits are described
  below.

- Your personal information will be kept confidential.

**PART 1: ABOUT THE STUDY**

**1. Why is this study being done?**

Colorectal cancer (cancer of the large bowel or colon) that has spread
to other parts of the body is called metastatic colorectal cancer. About
4-5 out of every 100 people with metastatic colorectal cancer have a
feature called dMMR (deficient mismatch repair) or MSI-H (microsatellite
instability-high). This special feature means that the cancer has many
mutations (changes in its DNA) and may respond well to a type of
treatment called immunotherapy, which helps your own immune system fight
the cancer.

Cervimab is an investigational drug that works by blocking a \'brake\'
on the immune system called PD-1. By releasing this brake, Cervimab may
allow your immune system to better attack the cancer. Early studies
suggest Cervimab may work well in people whose cancer has the dMMR/MSI-H
feature.

The purpose of this Phase 3 study is to find out whether Cervimab is
better than the current standard chemotherapy treatment (called FOLFOX-4
plus bevacizumab) in people with your type of cancer. About 480 patients
across approximately 120 research centers in 18 countries will take
part.

**2. What is Cervimab?**

Cervimab is an antibody --- a protein made in the laboratory --- that
attaches to a molecule called PD-1 on certain immune cells. PD-1
normally acts like a \'brake\' that prevents immune cells from attacking
cancer. By blocking PD-1, Cervimab may allow your immune cells to
recognize and destroy cancer cells more effectively.

Cervimab is not yet approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration
(FDA) or other health authorities. It has been tested in earlier (Phase
1 and Phase 2) studies with approximately 180 patients. Based on those
results, researchers believe it may benefit patients with dMMR/MSI-H
metastatic colorectal cancer.

**3. What is the current standard treatment?**

The current standard first treatment for metastatic colorectal cancer is
a combination of chemotherapy drugs called FOLFOX-4 (oxaliplatin,
leucovorin, and 5-fluorouracil) plus a targeted drug called bevacizumab.
This treatment is given through an IV and requires frequent clinic
visits. It has known benefits and risks, described in Section 7 of this
document.

**PART 2: STUDY PROCEDURES**

**4. What will happen to me if I take part?**

**4.1 Screening (Up to 28 days before treatment starts)**

If you are interested in taking part, you will first have a
\'Screening\' period where we check whether you are eligible. These
tests may include:

- Physical examination and review of your medical history

- Blood and urine tests

- CT scan or MRI scan of your chest, abdomen, and pelvis

- Heart tracing (ECG)

- Tumor tissue testing (from a biopsy or surgical specimen you have
  already had) to confirm the dMMR/MSI-H feature

- Pregnancy test (if you are a woman who could become pregnant)

Some of these tests may be done as part of your routine cancer care. If
the tests show you cannot take part, we will explain why. Being found
ineligible will not affect your access to standard cancer treatment.

**4.2 Randomization (Day 1)**

If you are eligible, you will be randomly assigned (like flipping a
coin, managed by a computer system) to one of two treatment groups. You
have an equal 50% chance of receiving either treatment:

- Group A (Experimental): Cervimab given as an IV infusion once every 3
  weeks

- Group B (Standard): FOLFOX-4 chemotherapy plus bevacizumab given as IV
  infusions every 2 weeks

This study is double-blind: neither you nor your study doctor will know
which group you are in until the study is complete. Both groups will
also receive \'dummy\' (placebo) infusions to keep the treatment
appearance the same between groups. The study team knows which group you
are in for safety reasons, if needed.

**4.3 Treatment Period**

Treatment will continue as long as it is helping you and side effects
are manageable, for up to 2 years. You will visit the clinic regularly.
During visits you will receive study treatment, have physical exams,
blood tests, and other assessments. Clinic visits will vary in frequency
(typically every 2-3 weeks during active treatment).

**4.4 Tumor Scans**

CT or MRI scans to check the cancer will be done every 6 weeks for the
first year and every 12 weeks thereafter. This is to monitor how your
cancer is responding to treatment.

**4.5 End of Treatment**

When you finish study treatment (for any reason), you will have a final
\'End of Treatment\' visit within 30 days. We will check your health and
do blood tests. You will then be followed up for safety for 90 days
after your last dose.

**4.6 Long-Term Follow-Up**

After your safety follow-up, we will contact you every 3 months (by
phone or clinic visit) to check your health and whether you are still
alive. This may continue for several years.

**5. How long will I be in the study?**

Your active treatment may last up to 2 years. After treatment ends, your
participation in follow-up visits may continue for several years. The
total length of the entire study is expected to be approximately 4 years
from when the first patient enrolls.

**6. What tissue samples are needed?**

The study requires a sample of your tumor tissue. This is usually taken
from a biopsy or surgery you have already had (archival tissue). We ask
that you provide an archived tissue block or slides. A fresh biopsy is
encouraged but not required. Tumor tissue will be tested at a central
laboratory to confirm your eligibility and to learn more about how your
cancer responds to treatment. A blood sample will also be collected at
several time points for research purposes (pharmacokinetics,
immunogenicity, biomarkers).

**PART 3: RISKS AND BENEFITS**

**7. What are the possible risks and discomforts?**

  -----------------------------------------------------------------
  **All research involves some risk. The risks listed below are not
  exhaustive. Unexpected risks may be discovered during the course
  of the study.**

  -----------------------------------------------------------------

**7.1 Risks of Cervimab (Immunotherapy)**

Cervimab works by activating your immune system. This can sometimes
cause the immune system to attack normal body tissues, which is called
an immune-related adverse reaction (irAR). These reactions can affect
many different parts of the body and can range from mild to severe. The
most common and important ones include:

- Lung inflammation (pneumonitis): cough, shortness of breath, fever ---
  may be serious

- Bowel inflammation (colitis): diarrhea, abdominal pain --- may be
  serious

- Liver inflammation (hepatitis): detected on blood tests --- may
  require stopping treatment

- Skin reactions: rash, itching --- usually mild

- Thyroid problems: may need long-term thyroid medication

- Adrenal gland or pituitary gland problems: fatigue, dizziness,
  hormonal changes

- Kidney inflammation (nephritis): detected on blood tests

- Infusion reactions: chills, fever, flushing, rash during or after IV
  infusion

Most immune-related reactions can be treated effectively with
corticosteroids (such as prednisone). In rare cases, severe or fatal
immune-related reactions have occurred.

**7.2 Risks of FOLFOX-4 Chemotherapy**

If you are assigned to the standard chemotherapy arm, you may
experience:

- Nausea, vomiting, and loss of appetite

- Fatigue and weakness

- Tingling or numbness in hands and feet (peripheral neuropathy ---
  common with oxaliplatin)

- Increased risk of infection (low white blood cell count)

- Hair thinning or loss

- Mouth sores

- Risk of blood clots

**7.3 Risks of Bevacizumab**

- High blood pressure

- Protein in the urine

- Impaired wound healing

- Rare risk of perforation (hole) in the bowel or stomach

- Risk of stroke or heart attack

**7.4 Risks of Blood Draws**

Blood draws may cause minor discomfort, bruising, or rarely infection at
the needle site.

**7.5 Risks of CT Scan**

CT scans use X-ray radiation, which carries a small risk. You will be
exposed to CT scans more frequently than in routine clinical practice.
The contrast dye used may rarely cause allergic reactions or kidney
problems.

**7.6 Reproductive Risks**

Cervimab and chemotherapy drugs may harm an unborn baby. If you are
pregnant or become pregnant during the study, you must stop treatment
immediately and tell your study doctor. Women of childbearing potential
and male patients with female partners who could become pregnant must
use highly effective contraception for the duration of the study and for
6 months after the last dose.

**8. What are the possible benefits of taking part?**

You may or may not directly benefit from taking part in this study.
Based on earlier studies, there is hope that Cervimab may control your
cancer better than standard chemotherapy. However, there is no guarantee
that the investigational treatment will help you personally.

Information learned from this study may help future patients with the
same type of cancer. Your participation will contribute to scientific
knowledge about how to treat dMMR/MSI-H metastatic colorectal cancer.

**9. Are there other treatments available?**

If you decide not to join this study, other treatment options are
available. You will not lose access to standard cancer care. Your doctor
can discuss all your options with you, including currently approved
treatments and other clinical studies. If you decide to join and later
choose to leave, you can still receive standard care.

**10. How will my personal information be protected?**

Your privacy and confidentiality are very important to us. Information
collected about you as part of this study will be kept strictly
confidential. Your name and personal identifying information will never
be used in any publication or report of this study. You will be
identified only by a coded number.

Your study records may be reviewed by:

- The Sponsor, Oncobiologics, Inc., and its authorized representatives

- The IRB/IEC that approved this study

- The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA), the European Medicines
  Agency (EMA), and other health authorities in countries where this
  study is conducted

- Study monitors and auditors

These parties are required to maintain your information in confidence.
Your records will be kept for at least 15 years after the study ends, as
required by regulation.

By signing this consent form, you permit the uses and disclosures of
your health information described above for the purposes of this
research study, in accordance with the Health Insurance Portability and
Accountability Act (HIPAA) and applicable local privacy laws.

**11. What will happen to my samples?**

Tumor tissue and blood samples collected for this study will be used for
the tests described in this consent form. Leftover samples may be stored
for future research related to cancer and immune system conditions.
Future research on your samples may be conducted without your additional
consent unless required by applicable law. If you do not agree to the
future use of your samples, you may still participate in the main study
but must note your preference on the consent form.
