**2. INTRODUCTION**

**2.1 Disease Background**

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the third most commonly diagnosed malignancy
and the second leading cause of cancer-related mortality globally, with
an estimated 1.9 million new cases and 935,000 deaths in 2022 (GLOBOCAN
2022). Approximately 20-25% of patients present with de novo metastatic
disease, and an additional 25-30% of initially localized CRC patients
eventually develop metastatic disease. Despite advances in chemotherapy
regimens and targeted agents, median overall survival in first-line mCRC
ranges from 24 to 32 months.

Deficient mismatch repair (dMMR) and high microsatellite instability
(MSI-H) is identified in approximately 4-5% of mCRC cases and represents
a distinct molecular subtype characterized by high tumor mutational
burden (TMB; typically \> 40 mut/Mb), abundant tumor-infiltrating
lymphocytes (TILs), and unique immune evasion mechanisms. The PD-1/PD-L1
pathway is a dominant mechanism of immune suppression in dMMR/MSI-H
tumors, providing a compelling rationale for PD-1 checkpoint inhibitor
therapy.

Pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1) demonstrated superior PFS vs. chemotherapy in
KEYNOTE-177 (HR 0.60, p = 0.0002) and received FDA approval for
first-line dMMR/MSI-H mCRC in 2020. However, a substantial proportion of
patients (approximately 40%) experience early progression on
pembrolizumab, and OS benefit in the ITT population was marginal (HR
0.74). Cervimab, with its distinct epitope binding, superior receptor
occupancy kinetics, and potent co-inhibitory receptor blockade profile,
offers a potentially improved therapeutic option.

**2.2 Investigational Product**

**Drug Name:** Cervimab (NPA-PD1-001)

**Type:** Fully human IgG4 monoclonal antibody targeting PD-1 (CD279)

**Mechanism:** Blocks PD-1 interaction with PD-L1 and PD-L2, restoring
T-cell effector function

**Route of Administration:** Intravenous (IV) infusion over 30 minutes

**Dose:** 400 mg Q4W (selected based on Phase 1/2 PK/PD modeling;
CERV-101, CERV-201)

**Formulation:** 10 mg/mL concentrated solution; no preservatives;
refrigerated storage (2-8 degrees C)

**Manufacturer:** NovaPharma Biologics LLC, Durham, NC (BLA facility
registered; 21 CFR Part 600)

Phase 1/2 studies in solid tumors (CERV-101: n = 210; CERV-201: n = 155
CRC-enriched cohort) established the 400 mg Q4W dose, characterized the
PK profile (t1/2 approximately 26 days), and demonstrated preliminary
efficacy signals in dMMR/MSI-H CRC (ORR 49%, median PFS 19.4 months,
18-month OS rate 72%). No new safety signals were identified relative to
the anti-PD-1 class.

**4. INVESTIGATIONAL PLAN**

**4.1 Overall Study Design**

CERV-301 is a Phase 3, multicenter, open-label, 1:1 randomized,
active-controlled study. The study enrolled patients at 87 investigative
sites across 18 countries in North America, Europe, and Asia-Pacific.
Following eligibility verification, all patients were centrally
randomized via IWRS (Medidata Rave RTSM) in a 1:1 ratio to cervimab (Arm
A) or mFOLFOX-6 plus bevacizumab (Arm B). Randomization was stratified
by ECOG performance status (0 vs. 1-2), geographic region (North
America/Western Europe vs. rest of world), and prior adjuvant
chemotherapy (yes vs. no).

Cervimab was administered as a 400 mg IV infusion over 30 minutes every
4 weeks. mFOLFOX-6 was administered on Days 1 and 15 of each 28-day
cycle (Q2W) alongside bevacizumab 5 mg/kg IV Q2W. Treatment continued
until confirmed progression per RECIST v1.1, unacceptable toxicity,
withdrawal of consent, or other protocol-defined discontinuation
criteria.

**4.2 Selection of Study Population**

**4.2.1 Inclusion Criteria**

- Adults \>= 18 years of age

- Histologically or cytologically confirmed adenocarcinoma of the colon
  or rectum with documented metastatic disease

- dMMR/MSI-H confirmed by local IHC (MMR protein loss) and/or central
  PCR/NGS testing (Exact Sciences MSI Complete assay)

- No prior systemic therapy for metastatic CRC (adjuvant chemotherapy
  completed \>= 6 months prior to enrollment is allowed)

- ECOG performance status 0-2

- Measurable disease per RECIST v1.1 (at least one lesion \>= 10 mm on
  CT/MRI)

- Adequate organ function (ANC \>= 1.5 x 10\^9/L; platelets \>= 100 x
  10\^9/L; Hgb \>= 9.0 g/dL; creatinine clearance \>= 30 mL/min; AST/ALT
  \<= 2.5 x ULN; total bilirubin \<= 1.5 x ULN)

- Life expectancy \>= 12 weeks

- Women of childbearing potential: negative serum pregnancy test;
  willing to use effective contraception

**4.2.2 Exclusion Criteria**

- Prior treatment with any anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1, anti-CTLA-4, or other
  immune checkpoint inhibitor

- Active autoimmune disease requiring systemic immunosuppressive therapy
  within the past 2 years

- Active or prior documented inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn\'s
  disease or ulcerative colitis)

- History of severe hypersensitivity (Grade \>= 3) to any monoclonal
  antibody

- Active hepatitis B or C infection; HIV with viral load \> 50 copies/mL

- Interstitial lung disease or active, non-infectious pneumonitis

- Uncontrolled hypertension (BP \> 150/100 mmHg on \>= 2 occasions) or
  arterial thromboembolic event within 6 months

- Brain metastases, unless asymptomatic and stable for \>= 4 weeks off
  corticosteroids

- Pregnancy or breastfeeding

- Active second malignancy requiring treatment (exceptions: adequately
  treated basal/squamous cell carcinoma, in-situ cervical carcinoma)

**4.3 Treatments Administered**

**4.3.1 Arm A --- Cervimab**

Cervimab 400 mg in 250 mL 0.9% NaCl, administered as a 30-minute IV
infusion on Day 1 of each 28-day cycle. Pre-medication (antihistamine
and acetaminophen) was optional per institutional practice. Dose
modifications were not permitted; dose delays of up to 6 weeks for
toxicity management were allowed. Treatment was continued until
progression, intolerable toxicity, or patient withdrawal.

**4.3.2 Arm B --- mFOLFOX-6 plus Bevacizumab**

mFOLFOX-6: oxaliplatin 85 mg/m2 IV over 2 hours, leucovorin 400 mg/m2 IV
over 2 hours, 5-fluorouracil 400 mg/m2 IV bolus, then 5-fluorouracil
2400 mg/m2 IV over 46 hours, on Days 1 and 15 of each 28-day cycle.
Bevacizumab 5 mg/kg IV over 30-90 minutes on Days 1 and 15. Dose
modifications for toxicity were permitted per protocol Appendix D.
Oxaliplatin discontinuation for cumulative neuropathy was allowed with
continued 5-FU/leucovorin and bevacizumab.

**4.4 Randomization and Blinding**

Due to the different routes, schedules, and administration requirements
of the two regimens, the study was conducted in an open-label design.
However, all tumor response assessments were performed by BICR
(Bio-Imaging Research Inc., Wayne, PA) whose personnel were blinded to
treatment assignment and all clinical data. An Independent Data
Monitoring Committee (IDMC) monitored safety and interim efficacy data
in a blinded manner. The primary and key secondary endpoints were
evaluated by BICR.

**6.4 Subgroup Analyses --- PFS Forest Plot Summary**

PFS benefit favored cervimab across all pre-specified subgroups.
Notably, the benefit was consistent regardless of PD-L1 CPS status, TMB
level (high vs. low), ECOG performance status, geographic region, and
KRAS/NRAS/BRAF mutational status. Patients with BRAF V600E mutations (n
= 86) had the most pronounced PFS benefit (HR 0.28, 95% CI: 0.16, 0.47),
consistent with the known biology of this high-TMB subset. No
statistically significant treatment-by-subgroup interaction was detected
for any pre-specified subgroup (all interaction p-values \> 0.10).

**6.5 Health-Related Quality of Life**

Time to definitive clinically meaningful deterioration (TTCD) in EORTC
QLQ-C30 global health status was significantly prolonged with cervimab
(median not reached) versus mFOLFOX-6/bevacizumab (median 9.3 months);
HR = 0.51 (95% CI: 0.36, 0.71; p \< 0.0001). Cervimab-treated patients
showed significantly better preservation of physical functioning (p =
0.003), role functioning (p = 0.018), and significantly lower fatigue
scores (p \< 0.0001) and nausea/vomiting (p \< 0.0001) compared to the
control arm. EQ-5D-5L utility index was significantly higher in the
cervimab arm from Week 8 onward (all p \< 0.05 by MMRM).

**7.4 Immune-Mediated Adverse Events**

Immune-mediated AEs (imAEs) of any grade were reported in 70
cervimab-treated patients (32.1%) versus 6 (2.8%) in the control arm.
The most common imAEs in the cervimab arm were colitis (11.9%, Grade
3-4: 4.6%), hypothyroidism (18.3%, all Grade 1-2), and pneumonitis
(6.4%, Grade 3-4: 2.8%). Systemic corticosteroids were required in 54
patients (24.8%) in the cervimab arm for imAE management;
steroid-sparing agents (infliximab or mycophenolate mofetil) were
required in 8 patients (3.7%). Median time to resolution of Grade \>= 3
imAEs was 6.4 weeks (range: 2-28 weeks). No patients developed Grade 4-5
colitis or hepatitis; two deaths were adjudicated as treatment-related
(Grade 5 pneumonitis: n=1; Grade 5 myocarditis: n=1).

**7.5 Deaths**

A total of 186 deaths occurred during the study (71 cervimab; 115
control). The majority of deaths were due to progressive disease (67
cervimab; 107 control). Three deaths were adjudicated as
treatment-related: 2 in the cervimab arm (1 Grade 5 pneumonitis; 1 Grade
5 myocarditis) and 1 in the control arm (Grade 5 sepsis secondary to
febrile neutropenia). All treatment-related deaths were reviewed by the
IDMC and Medical Monitor. No clustering of specific AEs preceding death
was identified.

**8. PHARMACOKINETICS AND PHARMACODYNAMICS**

**8.1 Pharmacokinetics**

Cervimab PK was characterized in 211 patients with evaluable PK data.
Steady-state serum concentrations were achieved by Cycle 3 (Week 8),
consistent with the observed half-life of approximately 26-28 days. Mean
(CV%) steady-state trough concentration (Ctrough,ss) was 42.8 mcg/mL
(48.2%), and mean peak concentration (Cmax,ss) was 312.7 mcg/mL (31.4%).
PK parameters were consistent with prior Phase 1/2 studies and exhibited
low intra-patient variability over time. Body weight was identified as a
statistically significant but clinically minor covariate; no dose
adjustment was warranted.

**8.2 Pharmacodynamic Biomarkers**

PD-1 receptor occupancy on circulating CD4+ and CD8+ T cells exceeded
98% at all post-dose time points assessed (Week 2, 4, and 8), confirming
complete and sustained target engagement at the selected dose. Baseline
PD-L1 CPS was evaluated as a biomarker of efficacy; while CPS \>= 1 and
CPS \>= 10 subgroups showed numerically greater PFS benefit (HR 0.33 and
0.29, respectively), PFS benefit was also observed in the CPS \< 1
subgroup (HR 0.52, 95% CI: 0.26, 1.02), consistent with the observation
that dMMR/MSI-H is the dominant predictive biomarker regardless of PD-L1
expression.

On-treatment increases in intratumoral CD8+ TIL density (available in 47
paired biopsies) were significantly greater in responding patients
(median +4.2-fold from baseline) versus non-responders (+1.1-fold; p =
0.0003). ctDNA clearance by Week 8 was achieved in 61.4% of responding
cervimab patients and was associated with significantly improved PFS (HR
for ctDNA clearance vs. non-clearance: 0.34, 95% CI: 0.22, 0.52).

**9. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS**

**9.1 Efficacy Summary**

The primary analysis of CERV-301 demonstrated highly statistically
significant and clinically meaningful superiority of cervimab over the
standard-of-care mFOLFOX-6 plus bevacizumab regimen in patients with
previously untreated dMMR/MSI-H metastatic CRC. The primary endpoint was
met with a hazard ratio for PFS of 0.38 (95% CI: 0.29, 0.51; p \<
0.0001), representing a 62% reduction in the risk of disease progression
or death. The magnitude of PFS benefit substantially exceeds the
protocol-assumed HR of 0.55, reflecting a highly active agent in this
molecularly defined population.

Key secondary endpoints further support the clinical benefit of
cervimab: OS was significantly improved (HR 0.61; p = 0.0037) despite
immaturity of OS data and significant post-progression therapy crossover
in the control arm. Complete response rates were more than 3-fold higher
with cervimab (12.7% vs. 3.6%), and durable responses were substantially
more frequent (DoR \>= 24 months in 48.6% of cervimab responders vs.
4.8% with mFOLFOX-6). Patient-reported outcomes consistently favored
cervimab across all functional and symptom domains.

Subgroup analyses demonstrated consistency of PFS benefit across all
pre-specified subgroups, including patients who are traditionally
considered poor-prognosis (BRAF V600E mutant, ECOG PS 1-2, right-sided
colon cancer, liver metastases), supporting the broad applicability of
cervimab in the dMMR/MSI-H first-line mCRC setting.

**9.2 Safety Summary**

Cervimab demonstrated a safety profile consistent with the anti-PD-1
drug class and was meaningfully better tolerated than mFOLFOX-6 plus
bevacizumab for key chemotherapy-specific toxicities (peripheral
neuropathy, neutropenia, nausea, hypertension). The immune-mediated AE
profile (32.1% any grade; Grade 3-4 in approximately 12%) is consistent
with approved anti-PD-1 agents and was manageable with established
algorithms. Two treatment-related deaths occurred in the cervimab arm
(0.9%), consistent with class effects (pneumonitis, myocarditis); both
had been identified in previous cervimab studies and were specifically
monitored under the Cervimab Immune-Mediated Toxicity Management Guide.

The benefit-risk profile of cervimab is highly favorable in the
dMMR/MSI-H mCRC first-line setting, with substantially greater efficacy
accompanied by lower rates of Grade 3-4 TRAEs (28.4% vs. 71.4%),
superior treatment tolerability (relative dose intensity \>= 85% in
83.5% vs. 65.0%), and better preservation of quality of life compared to
the chemotherapy/biologic standard of care.

**9.3 Conclusions**

Cervimab 400 mg Q4W demonstrated statistically significant superiority
over mFOLFOX-6 plus bevacizumab in PFS (primary endpoint), OS, ORR, DoR,
and patient-reported HRQoL in patients with previously untreated
dMMR/MSI-H metastatic CRC. The safety profile was consistent with the
anti-PD-1 class, manageable, and favorable relative to the comparator.
These results from CERV-301 support the submission of a Biologics
License Application (BLA) to the FDA for the approval of cervimab as a
first-line treatment for patients with dMMR/MSI-H metastatic colorectal
cancer, and position cervimab as a potential new standard of care in
this biomarker-defined population.
