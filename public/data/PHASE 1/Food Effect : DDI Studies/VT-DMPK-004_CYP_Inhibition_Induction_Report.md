**EXECUTIVE SUMMARY**

This report presents the results of a comprehensive in vitro drug--drug
interaction (DDI) assessment of cervimab, a fully human IgG4 monoclonal
antibody targeting the Programmed Cell Death Protein 1 (PD-1) receptor.
Studies were conducted in accordance with the U.S. FDA Guidance for
Industry: In Vitro Drug Interaction Studies --- Cytochrome P450 Enzyme-
and Transporter-Mediated Drug Interactions (January 2020) and ICH M12:
Drug Interaction Studies (2023), under 21 CFR Part 58 GLP conditions.

Cervimab was evaluated for: (1) direct (reversible) inhibition of
CYP1A2, CYP2B6, CYP2C8, CYP2C9, CYP2C19, CYP2D6, and CYP3A4; (2)
time-dependent (mechanism-based) inhibition of CYP3A4 and CYP2D6; and
(3) mRNA induction of CYP1A2, CYP2B6, and CYP3A4 in primary human
hepatocytes.

**1. INTRODUCTION**

**1.1 Test Article**

Cervimab is a fully human immunoglobulin G4 (IgG4) monoclonal antibody
(mAb) directed against PD-1. It has a molecular weight of approximately
148 kDa and contains the typical IgG4 structural features including the
S228P hinge stabilization mutation to prevent Fab-arm exchange. Cervimab
is formulated as a sterile liquid solution for IV infusion (150 mg/mL).

As a large-molecule biologic, cervimab is not a substrate of cytochrome
P450 enzymes. It is eliminated primarily via protein catabolism
(intracellular degradation to amino acids and small peptides) and via
FcRn-mediated recycling, which prolongs its half-life to approximately
18 days. However, per FDA and ICH guidance, large molecules that
modulate immune signaling pathways may indirectly affect CYP enzyme
activity through cytokine-mediated mechanisms, and an in vitro
assessment is required as a first-tier evaluation.

**2. METHODS**

**2.1 CYP Inhibition Studies --- Direct (Reversible) Inhibition**

**2.1.1 Test System**

Pooled human liver microsomes (HLMs; 20 donors, mixed sex; lot
#\[XXXXX\]; \[Vendor name\]) were used at a protein concentration of 0.1
mg/mL. Reactions were conducted in 100 mM potassium phosphate buffer (pH
7.4) supplemented with an NADPH-regenerating system (1 mM NADP+, 10 mM
glucose-6-phosphate, 1 U/mL glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase, 10 mM
MgCl₂).

Incubation conditions: 37°C, 10 minutes pre-incubation, 10-minute
reaction (CYP3A4 midazolam probe) to 30 minutes depending on probe,
terminated with ice-cold acetonitrile (1:2 v/v) containing internal
standard. Metabolites quantified by validated LC-MS/MS.

IC50 determination: Four-parameter logistic (4PL) nonlinear curve
fitting using GraphPad Prism v9. Inhibition Ki estimated from
IC50/(\[S\]/Km + 1). R\[DDI1\] calculated as 1 + \[I\]max,u / Ki where
\[I\]max,u = unbound Cmax (fu,p × 80 μg/mL; fu,p = 0.995 for IgG4 mAb).

**2.2 Time-Dependent Inhibition (TDI) Study Design**

CYP3A4 and CYP2D6 were evaluated for TDI using the shift in IC50
approach (±NADPH preincubation). If a ≥1.5-fold shift in IC50 is
observed in the presence vs. absence of NADPH preincubation, the test
article is considered to have TDI potential and further kinact/KI
characterization is triggered.

Protocol: HLMs (1 mg/mL) were preincubated with cervimab (0--100 μg/mL)
±NADPH for 30 minutes at 37°C. Following preincubation, the mixture was
diluted 20-fold into the reaction matrix containing probe substrate
(midazolam 2 μM for CYP3A4; dextromethorphan 5 μM for CYP2D6) and fresh
NADPH-regenerating system. Metabolite formation was quantified as above.

**2.3 CYP Induction Studies in Primary Human Hepatocytes**

CYP1A2, CYP2B6, and CYP3A4 mRNA induction was evaluated in
sandwich-cultured primary human hepatocytes from three individual donors
(sex, age, and genotype information in Appendix B). Hepatocytes were
treated for 48 hours with cervimab (1, 10, 50, 100 μg/mL) or positive
controls (omeprazole 50 μM for CYP1A2; phenobarbital 1 mM for CYP2B6;
rifampicin 10 μM for CYP3A4) in triplicate. Gene expression was measured
by qRT-PCR (TaqMan), normalized to GAPDH housekeeping gene.

Cytotoxicity was assessed by LDH release and MTT assay at all cervimab
concentrations. Results confirmed no cytotoxicity at any tested
concentration.

**5.3 Induction Interpretation**

Maximum fold-induction of CYP1A2 (1.11-fold), CYP2B6 (1.13-fold), and
CYP3A4 (1.16-fold) were observed at the highest cervimab concentration
(100 μg/mL). All fold-induction values are substantially below the
regulatory thresholds (2-fold for CYP3A4/CYP2B6; 5-fold for CYP1A2).
Furthermore, the maximum induction response as a percentage of the
positive control response is well below 20% for all three CYPs:

- CYP1A2 induction as % of omeprazole response: 1.11/18.7 × 100 = 0.6%

- CYP2B6 induction as % of phenobarbital response: 1.13/8.3 × 100 = 1.6%

- CYP3A4 induction as % of rifampicin response: 1.16/22.1 × 100 = 0.5%

Conclusion: Cervimab does not induce CYP1A2, CYP2B6, or CYP3A4 at
clinically relevant concentrations. No further clinical CYP induction
assessment is required based on in vitro data.

**6. CYTOKINE-MEDIATED DDI RISK ASSESSMENT**

Although the in vitro data demonstrate that cervimab does not directly
inhibit or induce CYP enzymes, its mechanism of action as a PD-1
antagonist introduces the potential for indirect, cytokine-mediated CYP
modulation. Upon T-cell activation following PD-1 blockade,
pro-inflammatory cytokines including IL-6, IFN-γ, and TNF-α are released
systemically. These cytokines are known to suppress hepatic CYP3A4,
CYP1A2, CYP2C9, and CYP2C19 transcription, potentially decreasing
clearance of CYP-metabolized co-medications and increasing their plasma
exposures.

The magnitude of cytokine-mediated CYP suppression following cervimab
therapy cannot be quantitatively predicted from the in vitro data in
this report. Published literature on approved anti-PD-1 agents
(pembrolizumab, nivolumab) has not identified clinically significant
cDDI in population PK analyses; however, prospective data from a
dedicated clinical probe study remain the definitive standard.

Risk factors that increase cDDI probability in cervimab-treated patients
include: dMMR/MSI-H tumor types (associated with inherently high immune
activation), combination with chemotherapy, and concomitant use of
CYP-sensitive narrow-therapeutic-index drugs (e.g., warfarin,
tacrolimus, cyclosporine, certain statins).

Recommendation: Conduct the prospective clinical DDI study (Protocol
CRV-DDI-002) to characterize the in vivo cDDI magnitude for CYP3A4,
CYP1A2, and CYP2C9 at steady-state cervimab. Until clinical data are
available, prescribing guidance should note the potential for
cytokine-mediated modulation and recommend monitoring of co-medications
with narrow therapeutic indices that are sensitive CYP substrates.
