**1. SUMMARY OF CERVIMAB**

  --------------------------------------------------------------------
  **This IB summary provides a concise overview of the current state
  of knowledge regarding cervimab. Investigators are expected to read
  the full IB in its entirety before enrolling patients in cervimab
  clinical trials.**

  --------------------------------------------------------------------

Cervimab is an investigational fully human IgG4 monoclonal antibody that
binds with high specificity and affinity (KD = 0.32 nM) to the
Programmed Death-1 (PD-1) inhibitory receptor expressed on activated T
lymphocytes. By blocking PD-1 interactions with its ligands PD-L1 and
PD-L2, cervimab restores anti-tumor immune responses in
immunosuppressive tumor microenvironments.

Cervimab is being developed for the treatment of dMMR/MSI-H metastatic
colorectal cancer (mCRC), a molecularly defined subgroup (\~5% of mCRC)
characterized by a high somatic tumor mutational burden (TMB), abundant
tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), and robust responsiveness to
PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint inhibition. The IND currently supports a Phase II
study (ACME-CERV-CRC-001; n=87 evaluable) and a Phase III randomized
controlled trial (ACME-CERV-CRC-003; n=420 planned).

**2. INTRODUCTION --- DISEASE BACKGROUND AND UNMET MEDICAL NEED**

**2.1 Colorectal Cancer --- Epidemiology**

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the third most common cancer and second
leading cause of cancer-related mortality worldwide, with an estimated
1.93 million new cases and 935,000 deaths globally in 2022 (Sung et al.,
CA Cancer J Clin 2021). In the United States, approximately 153,020 new
cases are diagnosed and 52,550 deaths occur annually (Siegel et al., CA
Cancer J Clin 2023). Approximately 20-25% of patients present with
synchronous metastatic disease, and an additional 30% develop
metachronous metastases. The 5-year survival rate for metastatic CRC
remains approximately 14% despite advances in systemic therapy.

**2.2 dMMR/MSI-H Colorectal Cancer**

Deficient mismatch repair (dMMR) is caused by germline or somatic
inactivation of one or more DNA mismatch repair (MMR) genes: MLH1, MSH2,
MSH6, or PMS2. Microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) status,
detectable by PCR-based assays or next-generation sequencing (NGS), is
the functional consequence of dMMR and results in a characteristic
pattern of frameshift mutations at repetitive DNA sequences. dMMR/MSI-H
CRC accounts for approximately 4-5% of metastatic CRC (compared to
\~15-20% of early-stage CRC, where Lynch syndrome and MLH1 promoter
methylation are major drivers).

The dMMR/MSI-H phenotype is associated with:

- High somatic tumor mutational burden (TMB): median 40-50 mutations/Mb
  vs. 2-5 Mb in MSS CRC

- Abundant frameshift peptide-derived neoantigens that are highly
  immunogenic

- Dense tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) infiltration, particularly
  CD8+ cytotoxic T cells and CD4+ T helper cells

- High expression of immune checkpoints (PD-L1, PD-L2, CTLA-4) in both
  tumor cells and TILs --- compensatory immunosuppression driven by
  active immune activation

- Resistance to fluoropyrimidine-based chemotherapy (FOLFOX, FOLFIRI)
  --- a key difference from MSS CRC that makes first-line treatment
  selection critical

**2.3 Current Standard of Care and Unmet Need**

The introduction of pembrolizumab as first-line therapy for dMMR/MSI-H
mCRC (KEYNOTE-177) established PD-1 blockade as the preferred approach
for this patient population, demonstrating superior PFS (HR=0.60) over
chemotherapy. However, a substantial proportion of patients (\~57% at
12-month PFS) progress on or after first-line pembrolizumab, creating a
need for additional PD-1/PD-L1 blocking agents with potentially
differentiated profiles (different epitope binding, combination
regimens).

Cervimab\'s unique CDR sequences and epitope of engagement with PD-1
(non-overlapping with pembrolizumab and nivolumab binding regions by
epitope mapping) provide a scientific rationale for potential activity
in pembrolizumab-refractory patients --- a hypothesis currently under
clinical investigation in a dedicated cohort of Study ACME-CERV-CRC-001.

**4. NON-CLINICAL STUDIES**

**4.1 Pharmacology --- Mechanism of Action**

PD-1 is an inhibitory co-receptor expressed on activated T cells, B
cells, natural killer (NK) cells, and myeloid cells. Engagement of PD-1
by its ligands PD-L1 (B7-H1; expressed on tumor cells, macrophages,
dendritic cells) or PD-L2 (B7-DC; expressed primarily on
antigen-presenting cells) recruits SHP-1 and SHP-2 phosphatases,
dampening TCR signaling, inhibiting T cell proliferation, reducing
cytokine production, and promoting T cell exhaustion.

Cervimab binds to the IgV domain of human PD-1 (extracellular, residues
34-150) with KD = 0.32 nM, occupying a non-overlapping epitope with the
PD-L1 binding interface (epitope confirmed by X-ray crystallography and
HDX-MS). This binding prevents PD-L1 and PD-L2 interactions,
de-repressing T cell activity and restoring productive anti-tumor
immunity.

**7. SAFETY GUIDANCE --- IMMUNE-RELATED ADVERSE EVENTS (irAEs)**

**7.1 General irAE Principles**

Immune-related adverse events (irAEs) are the primary toxicity of
interest with cervimab and all PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint inhibitors. irAEs
reflect uncontrolled T cell-mediated autoimmune inflammation in various
organ systems. Key principles for the investigator:

- Any organ system can be affected. Maintain a high index of suspicion
  for irAEs in any patient on cervimab presenting with new symptoms.

- Early recognition and prompt intervention are critical. Delayed
  treatment significantly worsens outcomes, particularly for Grade 3-4
  irAEs and cardiac events.

- Corticosteroids are the mainstay of treatment for most irAEs.
  High-dose IV corticosteroids should be initiated without delay for
  Grade 3-4 events.

- Steroid-sparing immunosuppressants (mycophenolate mofetil, infliximab,
  IVIG, tacrolimus) are used for corticosteroid-refractory irAEs --- per
  organ-specific guidance.

- Most irAEs are reversible with prompt management. Fatal irAEs
  (myocarditis, fulminant hepatitis, toxic epidermal necrolysis) require
  immediate recognition and action.

- Do NOT administer infliximab for immune-mediated hepatitis or
  myocarditis --- risk of severe/fatal hepatotoxicity and
  cardiotoxicity.

- Permanently discontinue cervimab for Grade 4 irAEs in most organ
  systems (exceptions: endocrinopathies --- may continue with hormone
  replacement; dermatitis, if controlled).

**7.2 Immune-Mediated Colitis**

Immune-mediated colitis/enteritis is the most common Grade ≥3 irAE in
the cervimab program (6.4% Grade ≥3). Key features: new onset or
worsening diarrhea (≥4 stools/day above baseline = Grade 3); abdominal
pain; blood or mucus in stool. Onset typically Cycles 2-6. Distinguish
from infectious diarrhea (stool culture, C. difficile) before initiating
immunosuppression.

- Grade 1 (mild diarrhea, 1-3 extra stools/day): Symptomatic management;
  loperamide; maintain hydration; continue cervimab with increased
  monitoring

- Grade 2 (moderate; 4-6 extra stools/day; abdominal pain): WITHHOLD
  cervimab; prednisone 1 mg/kg/day PO; GI consult; colonoscopy if no
  improvement in 3-5 days; resume when Grade ≤1

- Grade 3-4 (severe; \>6 stools/day; bloody; fever; peritoneal signs):
  PERMANENTLY DISCONTINUE; methylprednisolone 1-2 mg/kg/day IV; GI
  consult; infliximab 5 mg/kg IV × 1-2 doses if no response to steroids
  in 3-5 days (OK for colitis, unlike cardiac/hepatic irAEs); endoscopy
  for histological confirmation

**7.3 Hepatitis and Hepatotoxicity**

Immune-mediated hepatitis presents as asymptomatic LFT elevation (most
common) or rarely symptomatic jaundice, malaise, and elevated direct
bilirubin. Monitor AST, ALT, and total bilirubin at baseline and q3
weeks (each cycle, Day 1).

- Grade 1 (AST/ALT 1--3×ULN): Continue cervimab; increase monitoring to
  q2 weeks; hepatology consult if persistent

- Grade 2 (3--5×ULN): WITHHOLD cervimab; prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day;
  resume when Grade ≤1

- Grade 3 (5--20×ULN): PERMANENTLY DISCONTINUE; methylprednisolone 1-2
  mg/kg/day IV; hepatology consult; liver biopsy if diagnosis unclear.
  DO NOT use infliximab (severe hepatotoxicity risk).

- Grade 4 (\>20×ULN): PERMANENTLY DISCONTINUE; urgent hepatology
  referral; liver transplant evaluation if acute liver failure

**7.5 Pneumonitis**

Immune-mediated pneumonitis (any grade: 10.2%; Grade 3-4: 3.2%)
typically presents with new or worsening cough, dyspnea, and hypoxia. CT
chest with high-resolution images is recommended for any suspected case
(ground-glass opacities, consolidation, or mixed pattern). Distinguish
from infection, disease progression, and radiation recall pneumonitis.

- Grade 1 (asymptomatic; radiographic only): Withhold cervimab; close
  monitoring; pulmonology consult; resume when fully resolved

- Grade 2 (symptomatic; new O2 requirement \<2L/min): WITHHOLD;
  prednisone 1 mg/kg/day; bronchoscopy/BAL if clinically indicated

- Grade 3-4 (severe symptoms; ICU-level oxygen; ventilator): PERMANENTLY
  DISCONTINUE; methylprednisolone 2 mg/kg/day IV; pulmonology/critical
  care consultation

**7.6 Endocrinopathies**

Thyroid dysfunction is the most common endocrinopathy (21.4% combined).
Onset can be primary hypothyroidism (most common), hyperthyroidism (can
precede hypothyroidism in thyroiditis), or rarely isolated hypophysitis
(adrenal insufficiency + central hypothyroidism).

- Hypothyroidism (TSH \>10 mIU/L or symptomatic): Levothyroxine
  replacement; continue cervimab

- Hyperthyroidism (TSH \<0.5 mIU/L): Beta-blocker for symptom control;
  endocrinology consult; typically self-limited (2-4 weeks); continue
  cervimab

- Adrenal insufficiency (morning cortisol \<10 µg/dL or failed
  stimulation test): Hydrocortisone replacement (20-30 mg/day in divided
  doses); stress-dose corticosteroids for procedures/illness; continue
  cervimab with hormone replacement
