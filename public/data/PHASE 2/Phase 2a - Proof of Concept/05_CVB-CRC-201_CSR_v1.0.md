**7. INTRODUCTION**

**7.1 Colorectal Cancer and the dMMR/MSI-H Subset**

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the third most common cancer and second
leading cause of cancer death globally. In 2025, approximately 154,270
new cases of CRC were diagnosed in the United States and approximately
52,900 deaths were attributed to the disease (American Cancer Society,
2025). Approximately 20-25% of patients present with metastatic disease
at diagnosis, and an additional 25-30% eventually develop metastatic CRC
(mCRC). The median overall survival with standard first-line
chemotherapy (FOLFOX or FOLFIRI ± targeted agents) is approximately
28-30 months, and outcomes remain poor in the second-line setting.

Deficient mismatch repair (dMMR) or microsatellite instability-high
(MSI-H) status --- determined by immunohistochemistry or molecular
testing --- occurs in approximately 3-5% of mCRC cases. This molecular
subtype is characterized by: (1) hypermutated genomes with high tumor
mutational burden (median TMB \~40 mutations/Mb vs. \~3-5 mutations/Mb
in proficient MMR \[pMMR\] tumors); (2) dense immune infiltrates,
particularly CD8+ cytotoxic T lymphocytes; (3) upregulation of immune
checkpoint molecules including PD-1, PD-L1, and CTLA-4; and (4) strong
association with Lynch syndrome in \~40% of cases. These biological
features render dMMR/MSI-H tumors uniquely sensitive to immune
checkpoint inhibition.

**7.2 Established Immunotherapy Landscape in dMMR/MSI-H mCRC**

The clinical significance of this subtype was established by Le et al.
(Science, 2017), who demonstrated that dMMR tumors across multiple
histologies responded to PD-1 blockade with pembrolizumab, leading to
the first tumor-agnostic FDA approval based on a biomarker. In mCRC
specifically: pembrolizumab received FDA approval as first-line
monotherapy (KEYNOTE-177, ORR \~44%, PFS benefit vs. chemotherapy) and
as second/later-line therapy (KEYNOTE-158, ORR \~33%); nivolumab
monotherapy (CheckMate 142, ORR \~31%) and nivolumab plus ipilimumab
(ORR \~55%) also received FDA approval in second/later-line settings.
Despite these advances, approximately 55-60% of dMMR/MSI-H mCRC patients
do not respond to PD-1 monotherapy, creating a continued unmet need for
novel or more potent PD-1-directed agents.

**7.3 Rationale for Cervimab Development**

Cervimab is a fully humanized IgG4 monoclonal antibody with an
engineered S228P stabilizing mutation that selectively blocks PD-1,
preventing its interaction with PD-L1 and PD-L2. Preclinical data
demonstrated: IC50 for T-cell activation = 0.8 nM (comparable to
pembrolizumab 2.6 nM); no detectable off-target binding across 50 immune
receptor cross-reactivity panel; favorable predicted human PK (half-life
\~25 days, Ctrough at 200 mg Q3W predicted to maintain near-complete
receptor occupancy throughout the dosing interval). In the Phase 1 study
(CVB-001, N=56), cervimab showed an acceptable safety profile with irAEs
consistent with class effects, no treatment-related deaths, and
anti-tumor activity in multiple solid tumor types. In the 8 dMMR/MSI-H
tumor patients enrolled in CVB-001, 3 confirmed objective responses were
observed (ORR 37.5%; 2 colorectal, 1 endometrial), providing
proof-of-concept justifying this Phase 2a study.

**9. INVESTIGATIONAL PLAN**

**9.1 Study Design**

CVB-CRC-201 was a Phase 2a, open-label, single-arm, multicenter study
employing a two-stage Simon Optimal design. Cervimab 200 mg was
administered as a 30-minute IV infusion every 3 weeks until confirmed
disease progression, unacceptable toxicity, patient withdrawal, or
completion of 32 cycles (\~24 months), whichever occurred first.

**9.2 Selection and Timing of Doses**

The dose of 200 mg IV Q3W was selected based on pharmacokinetic modeling
from the Phase 1 study (CVB-001) demonstrating that this flat dose
maintains receptor occupancy \>95% throughout the Q3W dosing interval in
\>90% of subjects, independently of body weight. This dose was the
established RP2D from CVB-001.

**9.3 Blinding**

This was an open-label study; patients, investigators, and sponsor were
not blinded to treatment. However, tumor response assessments were
performed by a Blinded Independent Central Review (BICR) conducted by an
independent radiology vendor (RadReview, Inc.) whose reviewers were
blinded to clinical information, investigator assessments, and
patient-level safety data.

**9.4 Prior and Concomitant Therapy**

All patients had received at least one prior line of systemic
chemotherapy for metastatic disease. The most common prior regimens were
FOLFOX (72.5%), FOLFIRI (55.0%), and CAPOX (32.5%). Prior bevacizumab
was received by 57.5% of patients; prior cetuximab or panitumumab by
10.0% (all RAS/RAF wild-type patients). No patient had received prior
immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy. Supportive care medications were
permitted throughout the study. Systemic corticosteroids \> 10 mg/day
prednisone equivalent were prohibited, except for irAE management.

**9.5 Treatment Compliance**

Overall treatment compliance was high. Forty patients received at least
one dose of cervimab. The median number of cycles administered was 10
(range: 1-32). The median total duration of cervimab treatment was 30.1
weeks (range: 2.1-97.4 weeks). Dose delays occurred in 35.0% (14/40) of
patients. No dose reductions occurred (not permitted per protocol). Four
patients (10.0%) discontinued cervimab due to adverse events.

In 17 confirmed responders, the median DoR was not reached (NR) at the
primary data cutoff (median follow-up: 14.2 months). At the time of
analysis, 3 of 17 responders (17.6%) had progressed and 1 had died
without confirmed progression (censored). Thirteen of 17 responders
(76.5%) remained in response at data cutoff.

- 6-month DoR rate: 88.2% (95% CI: 60.6%, 96.9%)

- 12-month DoR rate: 76.9% (95% CI: 46.2%, 91.4%)

- Median DoR: Not Reached (data immature; only 4 events)

The range of DoR among the 4 responders who had progressed or died was
4.7-13.2 months. The longest ongoing response at data cutoff was 18.3
months.

**11.5 Progression-Free Survival**

In the EE population (N=40), 22 PFS events (progressions or deaths) were
observed at the primary data cutoff.

- Median PFS: 8.3 months (95% CI: 5.6 months, NR)

- 6-month PFS rate: 62.5% (95% CI: 45.8%, 75.8%)

- 12-month PFS rate: 47.8% (95% CI: 31.9%, 62.2%)

PFS results in non-responders (SD as BOR) showed a median PFS of 5.6
months (95% CI: 3.2, 8.1), suggesting meaningful disease stabilization
even in the absence of confirmed objective response.

**11.6 Overall Survival**

OS data were immature at the primary data cutoff, with 10 deaths
observed (25.0%) and median follow-up of 14.2 months. The median OS had
not been reached.

- 6-month OS rate: 95.0% (95% CI: 82.1%, 98.7%)

- 12-month OS rate: 79.8% (95% CI: 63.7%, 89.4%)

OS data will be updated in a planned post-primary analysis at a minimum
of 24 months of median follow-up.

**11.8 Exploratory Biomarker Analyses**

PD-L1 CPS was evaluable in 36/40 patients (90%). There was a positive
trend between PD-L1 CPS and ORR (CPS \>= 10: 61.1%; CPS 1-\<10: 37.5%;
CPS \<1: 20.0%), though the 95% CIs overlap substantially given small
sample sizes. TMB \>= 10 mut/Mb was associated with numerically higher
ORR compared to TMB-L (46.4% vs. 20.0%), consistent with the known
biology of hypermutated dMMR tumors. ctDNA dynamics (evaluable in 34/40
patients) demonstrated that early molecular response (ctDNA decrease \>=
50% at Cycle 2) was associated with higher ORR (73.7% vs. 13.3% in those
without early ctDNA decline), suggesting potential for ctDNA as an early
on-treatment biomarker. These observations are exploratory and require
validation in larger studies.

**11.9 Patient-Reported Outcomes**

EORTC QLQ-C30 completion rates were \>80% through Cycle 8 and declined
thereafter with patient discontinuations. The Global Health Status / QoL
score was maintained or improved from baseline in responding patients at
Week 12 (mean change: +7.8 points vs. baseline, SD 15.3) and Week 24
(mean change: +11.2 points, SD 17.6), consistent with clinical benefit.
EQ-5D-5L utility index was stable through Week 24 in the overall
population (mean change: +0.03, SD 0.12). Full PRO analyses are
presented in Appendix 16.3.

**12.5 Deaths**

Four patients (10.0%) died during the study or within 30 days of the

**12.6 Discontinuation Due to Adverse Events**

Four patients (10.0%) permanently discontinued cervimab due to AEs:
Grade 4 pneumonitis (N=1), Grade 4 myocarditis (N=1), Grade 3 colitis
(recurrent; N=1), and Grade 3 peripheral neuropathy (N=1). Three of

**12.7 Laboratory Findings**

Hematologic: No Grade \>= 3 hematologic AEs were attributed to cervimab.
Grade 1-2 lymphopenia was observed in 22.5% of patients and was not
clinically significant. No Grade \>= 3 neutropenia was observed.

Hepatic: Transaminase elevations (ALT or AST) were the most common
laboratory irAEs, occurring in 15.0% of patients (all grades); 7.5% were
Grade 3 (ALT) and 5.0% Grade 3 (AST). All resolved with corticosteroids;
none met Hy\'s Law criteria for drug-induced liver injury (no patient
had concurrent hyperbilirubinemia with transaminase elevation).

Endocrine: TSH abnormalities (elevated or suppressed) were observed in
10/40 (25.0%) patients. Hypothyroidism (7/40, 17.5%) was the most common
endocrine irAE, treated with levothyroxine replacement in all cases. Two
patients developed adrenal insufficiency requiring long-term
hydrocortisone replacement.

**12.8 Pharmacokinetics**

PK data were available from 37/40 (92.5%) evaluable patients. At steady
state (Cycle 3), the geometric mean Ctrough was 28.4 microg/mL (CV%
34.2) and geometric mean Cmax was 142.6 microg/mL (CV% 28.7), consistent
with near-complete PD-1 receptor occupancy throughout the dosing
interval and in line with Phase 1 predictions. No clinically significant
accumulation was observed beyond Cycle 3. ADA-positive patients (2/37;
5.4%) did not show evidence of altered PK or reduced clinical activity.

**13. DISCUSSION AND OVERALL CONCLUSIONS**

**13.1 Efficacy**

This Phase 2a study demonstrated that cervimab 200 mg IV Q3W has
clinically meaningful antitumor activity in patients with previously
treated dMMR/MSI-H mCRC. The primary endpoint was met: an ORR of 42.5%
(17/40; 95% CI: 27.0%, 59.1%) was observed by BICR per RECIST v1.1,
exceeding the pre-specified Simon design threshold of 13 responses
(one-sided p=0.011). These results are consistent with the emerging
class evidence for PD-1 inhibitors in dMMR/MSI-H mCRC, compare favorably
to approved agents (pembrolizumab ORR \~33% in second-line, KEYNOTE-158;
nivolumab ORR \~31%, CheckMate 142), and provide the most compelling
data to date for cervimab in this indication.

The durability of response is particularly noteworthy. With a median
follow-up of 14.2 months, median DoR had not been reached and 76.5% of
responders remained in response at data cutoff. The 12-month PFS rate of
47.8% and 12-month OS rate of 79.8% in a heavily pretreated population
(55% having received \>= 2 prior lines) compare favorably to historical
control data from second-line chemotherapy (12-month OS \~50%) and align
with immunotherapy benchmarks in this biomarker-defined population.

Exploratory analyses suggest that PD-L1 CPS and TMB may further enrich
the responding population, though the overlapping CIs and small subgroup
sizes preclude definitive conclusions. Importantly, responses were
observed even in PD-L1 CPS \< 1 (ORR 20%) patients, consistent with the
established principle that dMMR/MSI-H status, rather than PD-L1
expression, is the primary predictive biomarker for this population. The
ctDNA dynamics data are particularly intriguing, showing that early
molecular response at Cycle 2 strongly predicts final ORR, warranting
prospective validation.

**13.2 Safety**

The safety profile of cervimab was consistent with the known class
effects of PD-1 inhibitors and was generally manageable with established
protocols. The overall TRAE rate (87.5%) and Grade \>= 3 TRAE rate
(27.5%) are consistent with the approved PD-1 inhibitors in comparable
settings. The irAE rate (55.0%) and Grade \>= 3 irAE rate (17.5%) are
within the expected range for PD-1 inhibitors used as monotherapy in
oncology.

Importantly, no treatment-related deaths were observed. The four cases
of Grade \>= 3 serious irAEs (pneumonitis, myocarditis, recurrent
colitis, Grade 3 colitis) all resolved with appropriate
immunosuppression. The single case of myocarditis (Grade 4) was notable;
although rare (2.5%), this AE is a class effect of PD-1 inhibitors with
potentially fatal outcomes and requires vigilant monitoring and
immediate intervention. Two of the 4 patients who discontinued due to
AEs subsequently received alternative therapy and are in survival
follow-up.

**13.3 Benefit-Risk Assessment**

In the context of the significant unmet medical need in previously
treated dMMR/MSI-H mCRC --- a population with no approved second-line
immunotherapy options following progression on pembrolizumab or
nivolumab, and limited chemotherapy efficacy --- the benefit-risk
profile of cervimab 200 mg IV Q3W is favorable. The efficacy results
(ORR 42.5%, durable responses, meaningful PFS and OS data) in this
heavily pretreated population represent a clinically meaningful
treatment effect, and the safety profile is manageable with established
medical practices.

**13.4 Conclusions**

  -------------------------------------------------------------------
  **CONCLUSION: Cervimab 200 mg IV Q3W demonstrated clinically
  meaningful antitumor activity (ORR 42.5%; 95% CI: 27.0%, 59.1%)
  with durable responses and a manageable safety profile in patients
  with previously treated dMMR/MSI-H mCRC. The primary study
  objective was met (H0 rejected; p=0.011). These data support
  advancement of cervimab to a randomized Phase 2b/3 study in this
  indication.**

  -------------------------------------------------------------------

Based on these results, Cervi Therapeutics intends to: (1) initiate a
Phase 2b randomized study comparing cervimab to investigator\'s choice
of standard second-line therapy in dMMR/MSI-H mCRC patients who have
progressed on first-line PD-1 inhibitor-based therapy; (2) pursue
Breakthrough Therapy Designation from the FDA based on the ORR and
durability results; and (3) explore cervimab in combination with other
agents (anti-VEGF, anti-LAG3) in Phase 1b expansion cohorts.
