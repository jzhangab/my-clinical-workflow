**INTRODUCTION: WHY ARE YOU BEING ASKED TO PARTICIPATE?**

You are being invited to take part in a clinical research study because
you have been diagnosed with colorectal cancer that has spread to other
parts of your body (called metastatic colorectal cancer), and your tumor
has been shown to have a specific characteristic called dMMR or MSI-H
(explained below). Researchers at Cervi Therapeutics, Inc. are testing
an investigational drug called cervimab to see if it is safe and whether
it can help treat your cancer.

This document is called an Informed Consent Form (ICF). It explains the
purpose of the research, what will be asked of you, the risks and
benefits, your rights, and your alternatives. Participation in this
study is completely voluntary. You may choose not to participate, and if
you agree to participate, you may withdraw at any time without penalty
or loss of benefits to which you are entitled.

Please read this entire document carefully. The research staff will also
explain the study to you. Please ask questions about anything you do not
understand. Take as much time as you need --- do not sign this form
until all your questions have been answered and you feel comfortable
with your decision.

**WHAT IS THE PURPOSE OF THIS STUDY?**

The main purpose of this study is to find out whether cervimab, an
experimental drug that works by helping your immune system fight cancer,
is effective and safe for people with your type of colorectal cancer.

Cervimab is an antibody --- a type of protein --- that works by blocking
a \'brake\' on your immune system called PD-1. When PD-1 is blocked,
your immune cells may be better able to find and attack cancer cells.
Drugs that work this way are called \'immune checkpoint inhibitors.\'

Your cancer has a feature called dMMR (deficient mismatch repair) or
MSI-H (microsatellite instability-high). These terms mean that your
cancer cells have a problem with repairing their own DNA. As a result,
your tumor tends to have many mutations (changes in DNA) that may make
it more visible to your immune system --- and potentially more
responsive to drugs like cervimab.

This is an early-phase clinical trial. About 40 participants from
approximately 10-15 hospitals across the United States will participate
in this study.

**WHAT DOES PARTICIPATION IN THIS STUDY INVOLVE?**

**Study Drug and Administration**

If you decide to participate, you will receive cervimab as an
intravenous (IV) infusion --- meaning the drug will be delivered into
your vein through a needle. Each infusion will take approximately 30
minutes. Cervimab will be given every 3 weeks. One treatment cycle = 3
weeks.

You may continue to receive cervimab for up to about 2 years (24
months), as long as: (1) your cancer is not getting worse; (2) you are
not having serious side effects; and (3) you and your doctor decide it
is in your best interest to continue.

**Study Visits and Tests**

Participating in this study requires more visits to the clinic than you
would have outside of this study. Below is a summary of what to expect:

- Screening Visit (before starting treatment): Up to 4 weeks before your
  first dose. This visit will include blood tests, urine tests, an ECG
  (heart tracing), CT or MRI scan, review of your cancer records, and a
  physical examination. You may need to have a piece of your tumor
  tissue (biopsy) taken if a recent sample is not available.

- Treatment Visits (every 3 weeks): At each visit, you will receive your
  cervimab infusion, have blood tests, and be examined by the study
  doctor. Every 6 weeks (2 cycles), you will have a CT or MRI scan to
  check how your cancer is responding. You will also be asked to
  complete questionnaires about how you feel.

- End of Treatment Visit: When you stop receiving cervimab (for any
  reason), you will have a final set of tests including blood work and a
  scan.

- Safety Follow-Up Visit: About 30 days after your last cervimab dose,
  you will return for safety checks.

- Long-Term Follow-Up: After your study participation ends, the research
  team will contact you every 3 months (by phone or clinic visit) for up
  to 2 years to check on your health and survival.

**Optional Tumor Biopsy**

If your cancer progresses (gets worse) while on the study, you may be
asked (but are NOT required) to have an additional tumor biopsy. This is
for research purposes only and will not directly affect your treatment.

**WHAT ARE THE FORESEEABLE RISKS AND DISCOMFORTS?**

  -------------------------------------------------------------------
  **Cervimab is an experimental drug. Not all of its risks are known.
  The risks described below are based on data from the ongoing Phase
  1 clinical trial and from other similar drugs in its class. There
  may be unforeseen risks or side effects.**

  -------------------------------------------------------------------

**Common Side Effects of Cervimab (Seen in \>10% of Patients in Phase
1)**

- Fatigue (tiredness)

- Nausea

- Decreased appetite

- Skin rash or itching

- Diarrhea

- Joint or muscle pain

- Cough

- Fever

**Immune-Related Side Effects (Can Affect Any Organ --- Can Be
Serious)**

Because cervimab works by activating your immune system, it can
sometimes cause the immune system to attack healthy organs in your body.
These are called immune-related adverse events (irAEs). They can range
from mild to life-threatening and can affect multiple organs:

- Lungs: Pneumonitis (lung inflammation) --- symptoms: new or worsening
  cough, shortness of breath, chest pain. Can be severe or
  life-threatening.

- Liver: Hepatitis (liver inflammation) --- may cause yellowing of
  skin/eyes, dark urine, right-sided abdominal pain, elevated liver
  enzymes on blood tests.

- Intestines: Colitis (intestinal inflammation) --- symptoms: diarrhea
  (possibly with blood), abdominal pain, cramping.

- Endocrine Glands: Thyroid disorders (hypothyroidism, hyperthyroidism),
  adrenal insufficiency, type 1 diabetes --- may require lifelong
  hormone replacement.

- Skin: Severe rash, Stevens-Johnson syndrome (rare but life-threatening
  blistering rash).

- Kidney: Nephritis (kidney inflammation) --- may show up as changes in
  urine or blood tests.

- Nervous System: Neuropathy, encephalitis (brain inflammation).

- Heart: Myocarditis (heart muscle inflammation) --- rare but
  potentially life-threatening; symptoms: chest pain, shortness of
  breath, palpitations, irregular heartbeat.

IMPORTANT: If you experience any new or worsening symptoms at any time
during the study, contact the study team IMMEDIATELY. Do not wait for
your next scheduled visit. Immune-related side effects can worsen
quickly.

**Infusion-Related Reactions**

Some patients experience reactions during or shortly after the infusion,
including chills, fever, flushing, rash, low blood pressure, or
difficulty breathing. Study staff will monitor you closely during and
after infusions.

**Risks of Blood Draws**

Blood draws may cause minor discomfort, bruising, or, rarely, infection
at the puncture site.

**Risks of Scans**

CT scans involve low doses of radiation. MRI uses magnets (no radiation)
but may be unsuitable if you have certain metal implants. IV contrast
used in CT/MRI can rarely cause allergic reactions or kidney injury.

**Risk to Pregnancy**

  -------------------------------------------------------------------
  **DO NOT participate in this study if you are pregnant or
  breastfeeding. Cervimab may cause serious harm to an unborn baby or
  nursing infant. If you could become pregnant, you must use highly
  effective birth control during treatment and for 5 months after
  your last dose. If you become pregnant during the study, notify the
  study team immediately.**

  -------------------------------------------------------------------

**WHAT ARE THE POTENTIAL BENEFITS?**

You may or may not personally benefit from participating in this study.
Based on early Phase 1 data, cervimab showed anti-tumor activity in
patients with dMMR/MSI-H tumors, with 3 out of 8 patients experiencing
tumor shrinkage. However, there is no guarantee that cervimab will
benefit you specifically.

If cervimab is effective for you, potential benefits may include
shrinkage of your tumor, stabilization of your cancer, prolonged
survival, and/or improved quality of life. The information learned from
this study may help future patients with colorectal cancer.

**WHAT ARE YOUR ALTERNATIVES TO PARTICIPATION?**

Your participation in this study is entirely voluntary. If you choose
not to participate or withdraw at any time, you will not lose any
benefits to which you are otherwise entitled, and you will continue to
receive standard medical care.

Alternatives to study participation include: standard-of-care
chemotherapy options (e.g., FOLFIRI, FOLFOX, CAPOX with or without
targeted agents); approved immune checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab
or nivolumab, if you have not received these previously --- discuss with
your oncologist); other clinical trials for which you may be eligible;
and best supportive care focused on quality of life and symptom
management.

We encourage you to discuss all available options with your primary
oncologist and other physicians involved in your care.
