**1.4 Unmet Medical Need and Scientific Rationale**

**1.4.1 Disease Burden and Current Treatment Landscape**

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the third most common cancer globally and the
second leading cause of cancer-related mortality in the United States,
with an estimated 153,020 new cases and 52,550 deaths projected in 2023
(Siegel et al., CA Cancer J Clin, 2023). Metastatic CRC (mCRC) carries a
median overall survival (mOS) of approximately 24--30 months with
current standard-of-care (SOC) therapies including FOLFOX/FOLFIRI
combined with bevacizumab or cetuximab (RAS/BRAF wild-type).

The dMMR/MSI-H subtype accounts for approximately 3--5% of mCRC cases.
This molecular subtype is associated with a deficiency in the mismatch
repair (MMR) pathway, leading to a hypermutation phenotype and enhanced
neoantigen burden. Paradoxically, dMMR/MSI-H mCRC is characterized by
poor response to conventional chemotherapy but exhibits remarkable
sensitivity to immune checkpoint inhibition.

**1.4.2 Scientific Rationale for PD-1 Blockade in dMMR/MSI-H mCRC**

The PD-1/PD-L1 immune checkpoint pathway serves as a critical negative
regulatory mechanism that suppresses anti-tumor T-cell activity within
the tumor microenvironment (TME). dMMR/MSI-H tumors exhibit: (1)
elevated tumor mutational burden (TMB) generating immunogenic
neoantigens; (2) dense tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) infiltration;
and (3) marked upregulation of PD-L1 expression on tumor cells and
tumor-associated macrophages. These features render dMMR/MSI-H tumors
particularly susceptible to PD-1 blockade.

Cervimab was engineered to provide superior PD-1 binding affinity (KD =
0.82 nM vs. 1.45 nM for pembrolizumab in head-to-head Biacore assays),
reduced Fc-mediated effector function via the IgG4 isotype with S228P
stabilizing mutation, and optimized pharmacokinetics supporting Q3W
dosing. Cervimab demonstrated compelling preclinical anti-tumor activity
in dMMR-enriched syngeneic murine models with a favorable safety profile
in NHP toxicology studies.

**2.1 Summary of Key Information**

Cervimab is a humanized, IgG4 monoclonal antibody directed against human
PD-1 (CD279). The compound is being developed for the treatment of solid
tumors with demonstrated microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) or
mismatch repair deficiency (dMMR), with the lead indication being
metastatic colorectal cancer. This IB summarizes all relevant
preclinical and early clinical data available at the time of preparation
to enable investigators to make an informed assessment of risks and
benefits for their patients.

**2.2 Introduction and Background**

**2.2.1 Drug Class and Mechanism of Action**

The programmed cell death-1 (PD-1) receptor is a co-inhibitory receptor
expressed on activated T cells, natural killer (NK) cells, and B cells.
PD-1 interacts with two ligands: PD-L1 (CD274, B7-H1) and PD-L2 (CD273,
B7-DC). Engagement of PD-1 by its ligands within the tumor
microenvironment (TME) delivers an inhibitory signal that dampens T-cell
receptor (TCR)-mediated activation, induces T-cell exhaustion, and
impairs anti-tumor immunity. Tumors exploit this pathway to evade immune
surveillance.

Cervimab binds PD-1 with high affinity and specificity, blocking
interaction with both PD-L1 and PD-L2, thereby releasing the brakes on
T-cell activation and enabling cytotoxic CD8+ T cells to recognize and
eliminate tumor cells expressing neoantigens. The IgG4 subclass was
selected to minimize antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC)
against PD-1-bearing immune cells. The S228P Fc mutation stabilizes the
IgG4 hinge region, preventing Fab-arm exchange.

**2.3 Nonclinical Pharmacology**

**2.3.1 Primary Pharmacology --- In Vitro Studies**

Receptor Binding Kinetics: Cervimab was characterized using surface
plasmon resonance (SPR) Biacore T200 analysis. The equilibrium
dissociation constant (KD) was determined to be 0.82 nM for human PD-1
and 1.14 nM for cynomolgus monkey PD-1, with kon = 1.8 × 10⁵ M⁻¹s⁻¹ and
koff = 1.5 × 10⁻⁴ s⁻¹ for the human interaction.

Functional Blocking Assay: Using a cell-based PD-1/PD-L1 blocking ELISA
(HTRF) and Jurkat reporter cell assay, Cervimab blocked PD-L1:PD-1
binding with IC50 = 2.3 nM and PD-L2:PD-1 with IC50 = 3.1 nM.

T-Cell Activation: In mixed lymphocyte reaction (MLR) assays using
allogeneic dendritic cells and CD4+/CD8+ T cells, Cervimab enhanced IL-2
production (EC50 = 1.8 nM) and IFN-γ secretion (EC50 = 2.4 nM) in a
dose-dependent manner. Anti-CD3/CD28-stimulated human PBMC assays
confirmed augmented T-cell proliferation (Ki-67+) and cytolytic capacity
(granzyme B+).

Cytokine Release Assay (CRA): Cervimab was evaluated in a
regulatory-grade CRA using immobilized antibody conditions at 1, 10, and
100 µg/mL with normal human donor PBMCs (n=8 donors). No clinically
relevant cytokine release was observed above background at any
concentration tested, supporting a low risk of cytokine release syndrome
(CRS) in vivo.

**2.3.2 Primary Pharmacology --- In Vivo Studies**

Syngeneic Murine Tumor Models: Since Cervimab does not cross-react with
murine PD-1, a surrogate anti-mouse PD-1 antibody was used in syngeneic
tumor models to generate pharmacological proof-of-concept data.
Surrogate anti-mPD-1 (3 mg/kg, 10 mg/kg, IP, Q3D × 5) in MC38 (MSS) and
CT26-dMMR (engineered MSI-H) models demonstrated:

- CT26-dMMR model: Tumor growth inhibition (TGI) of 87.2% at 10 mg/kg
  vs. isotype control (p\<0.001); 3/10 complete responses (CRs) at 10
  mg/kg.

- Immunological correlates: Significant increase in tumor-infiltrating
  CD8+ T cells (IHC, p\<0.001), decreased regulatory T cells (Tregs,
  FoxP3+), and elevated intratumoral IFN-γ and granzyme B by gene
  expression profiling.

- Re-challenge study: CR mice demonstrated durable protective immunity
  upon re-challenge with CT26-dMMR cells (5/5 CR mice rejected
  re-challenge), consistent with immunological memory.

- MC38 (MSS) model: Modest TGI of 28.4% at 10 mg/kg --- supporting the
  dMMR/MSI-H patient selection strategy.

**2.3.3 Secondary Pharmacology and Safety Pharmacology**

Selectivity Panel: Cervimab was evaluated against a panel of 85 human
antigens including immunologically relevant checkpoint molecules
(CTLA-4, LAG-3, TIM-3, TIGIT, CD28, ICOS, BTLA, CD80, CD86, CD137) using
SPR. No off-target binding was detected (all KD \>1 µM or no binding
detected).

Safety Pharmacology (ICH S7A/S7B): Dedicated safety pharmacology studies
in cynomolgus monkeys evaluated cardiovascular (telemetry: HR, BP, ECG),
respiratory, and central nervous system (Irwin screen) parameters. No
adverse effects were observed. A hERG assay was not conducted (not
applicable for large molecules per ICH S7B).

**2.7 Summary of Risks and Benefits**

Based on the totality of preclinical evidence, the benefit-risk profile
of Cervimab in the proposed dMMR/MSI-H mCRC population is considered
favorable. The expected adverse effects are consistent with the
established class effects of PD-1 inhibitors, including immune-related
adverse events (irAEs) such as immune-mediated pneumonitis, colitis,
hepatitis, endocrinopathies, and dermatitis. These events are manageable
with established clinical protocols. The unmet medical need in
later-line dMMR/MSI-H mCRC and the compelling preclinical evidence of
tumor selectivity justify clinical evaluation.

**3.1.3 Upstream Manufacturing Process**

Cervimab DS is produced using a fed-batch bioreactor process in CHO-K1
cells. The manufacturing process consists of the following sequential
unit operations:

8.  Thaw and Recovery: WCB vial thaw into T-flask, recovery in
    proprietary serum-free, chemically defined media.

9.  Seed Train Expansion: Progressive expansion through spinner flasks
    (125 mL → 250 mL → 1L) and WAVE bioreactor bags (10 L → 50 L) over
    approximately 10--14 days.

10. Production Bioreactor: Inoculation of 2,000 L stirred tank
    bioreactor (STR); fed-batch culture with proprietary nutrient feed
    strategy; 14-day run duration; controlled process parameters (pH
    7.0--7.2, DO 30--50%, temperature 36.8--37.2°C).

11. Harvest: Centrifugation (continuous disc stack) followed by depth
    filtration (0.45 µm + 0.2 µm) to produce clarified harvest.

Typical DS titer: 4.2--5.8 g/L (batch records). Consistent with industry
norms for optimized CHO processes.

**4.1 Primary Pharmacology**

**4.1.1 Mechanism of Action --- Molecular and Cellular Basis**

PD-1 is expressed on the surface of activated T lymphocytes, regulatory
T cells (Tregs), natural killer (NK) cells, B cells, and monocytes
following activation. The PD-1 intracellular domain contains two
tyrosine-based motifs: the immunoreceptor tyrosine-based inhibitory
motif (ITIM) and immunoreceptor tyrosine-based switch motif (ITSM). Upon
PD-L1/PD-L2 engagement, Src-family kinase-mediated phosphorylation of
ITSM recruits SHP-1/SHP-2 phosphatases which dephosphorylate ZAP-70 and
CD28, attenuating TCR-downstream signaling (PI3K/AKT, RAS/MAPK,
PLCγ/PKCθ pathways) and suppressing T-cell proliferation, cytokine
production, and cytolytic function.

Cervimab blocks the PD-1 extracellular domain at the C\'D loop and FG
loop interface --- the region directly involved in PD-L1/PD-L2 docking
--- as confirmed by competitive binding ELISA and epitope mapping via
hydrogen-deuterium exchange mass spectrometry (HDX-MS). Blockade
prevents PD-1 signaling and restores effector T-cell function in vitro
and in vivo.

**4.2 Secondary and Safety Pharmacology**

**4.2.1 CNS Safety Pharmacology --- Irwin Battery (Non-GLP)**

A modified Irwin battery was incorporated into the single-dose
escalation NHP PK study. No clinically meaningful CNS effects (tremor,
ataxia, sedation, convulsions, altered reflexes) were observed at any
dose level (5, 25, 100 mg/kg IV) up to 72 hours post-dose.

**4.2.2 Cardiovascular Safety Pharmacology --- Telemetry (GLP)**

Cardiovascular safety was assessed in telemetered cynomolgus monkeys
(n=4/sex) receiving single IV doses of Cervimab at 0, 5, 50, and 150
mg/kg. Continuous ECG, heart rate, blood pressure, and body temperature
were monitored for 48 hours post-dose. No drug-related effects on QTc
interval (ΔQTcF \<5 ms at all doses), heart rate, systolic/diastolic BP,
or body temperature were observed. Findings are consistent with the
expected pharmacological activity of a non-FcγR-engaging IgG4 antibody.

**4.2.3 Respiratory Safety Pharmacology**

Respiratory rate and tidal volume were assessed in the NHP GLP
toxicology study. No drug-related respiratory effects were observed. A
plethysmography study was not conducted; this is considered acceptable
per ICH S7A guidance for large molecules with demonstrated lack of
respiratory toxicity in the toxicology program.

**4.3 Pharmacokinetics and Drug Metabolism**

**4.3.1 Single-Dose IV PK --- Cynomolgus Monkey (Non-GLP)**

Non-compartmental analysis (NCA) of serum PK profiles from a 3-dose
group NHP single-dose study confirmed linear, dose-proportional PK in
the 0.5--25 mg/kg range. Mean half-life of 14--17 days is consistent
with typical IgG4 FcRn-recycling kinetics. Low volume of distribution
(\~70 mL/kg) is consistent with restriction to vascular/lymphatic
compartments.

**4.3.2 Repeat-Dose PK --- 4-Week GLP NHP Study**

Serum PK samples were collected pre-dose and at defined timepoints
throughout the 4-week GLP toxicology study. Cervimab showed
dose-proportional exposure (Cmax and AUC) at 10, 50, and 150 mg/kg/week.
Accumulation ratio (Day 28/Day 1 AUC) was 2.8--3.2-fold, consistent with
the predicted accumulation based on t½ and Q1W dosing frequency. No
apparent target-mediated drug disposition was observed. ADA incidence
was 0% across all groups and timepoints.

**4.3.3 Distribution and Metabolism**

As a therapeutic IgG4 monoclonal antibody, Cervimab is expected to be
distributed primarily in blood and lymphatic tissues. Tissue
distribution studies were not conducted but are considered not required
at this stage per ICH S6(R1). Catabolism follows physiological
proteolysis of IgG molecules to amino acids and peptides. No dedicated
metabolism studies (CYP, DDI) are required for biologics per FDA/ICH
guidance. Drug-drug interaction potential via CYP enzymes is considered
negligible.

**4.4 Toxicology Program**

**4.4.1 Species Selection Rationale**

Cynomolgus monkey (Macaca fascicularis) was selected as the sole
toxicology species based on: (1) comparable binding affinity of Cervimab
to cynomolgus PD-1 (KD = 1.14 nM) versus human PD-1 (KD = 0.82 nM); (2)
high PD-1 sequence homology between cynomolgus and human PD-1 (\>96%
amino acid identity); (3) confirmation of pharmacological activity
(T-cell activation biomarkers) in cynomolgus PBMCs ex vivo; and (4) lack
of cross-reactivity with rodent PD-1. Use of a single species is
justified per ICH S6(R1) based on pharmacological relevance and is
consistent with the regulatory approach accepted for approved PD-1
inhibitors.

**5.5 Efficacy Assessments**

Tumor assessments will be performed per RECIST v1.1 using CT (preferred)
or MRI (if CT contraindicated) of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis.
Imaging schedule: Baseline (within 28 days of Day 1), then every 6 weeks
(±1 week) for the first 24 weeks, then every 12 weeks. Brain MRI at
baseline if clinically indicated.

Response categories: Complete Response (CR), Partial Response (PR),
Stable Disease (SD), Progressive Disease (PD), Not Evaluable (NE).
Response confirmation: CR and PR require confirmation on repeat imaging
≥4 weeks after initial response assessment.

Pseudoprogression management: Atypical response patterns (initial
apparent progression followed by response) may occur with immunotherapy.
Patients with ECOG PS deterioration attributed to disease progression
and/or clinical decline will be discontinued; patients with
stable/improving clinical status may continue treatment with repeat
confirmatory imaging 4--6 weeks after suspected initial PD.

**5.7 Pharmacokinetic and Pharmacodynamic Assessments**

Full PK sampling in Part A (Cycle 1 and Cycle 3 intensive) will enable
non-compartmental analysis (NCA) and development of a population PK
(PopPK) model. Sparse sampling in Part B will contribute to PopPK model
refinement. PopPK analysis will be performed using NONMEM v7.5.

PD assessments: PD-1 receptor occupancy (RO) on peripheral T cells (CD4+
and CD8+) will be measured by flow cytometry using an Fc-silent
anti-PD-1 antibody that competes with Cervimab binding. Target RO \>80%
is the PD target. Additional T-cell activation markers (Ki-67, granzyme
B, HLA-DR) will be assessed on PBMC samples. Tumor biopsies (mandatory
at baseline; optional on-treatment biopsy at Cycle 2 Day 1) will be
evaluated for PD-L1 (SP142 IHC assay, CPS scoring), CD8+ TIL density,
and mutational biomarkers (TMB, MSI confirmation).

**5.8 Statistical Considerations**

**5.8.1 Sample Size Rationale**

Part A: The 3+3 design with mTPI-2 overlay will enroll approximately
3--6 patients per dose cohort. A total of 18--30 patients across 5--6
cohorts are expected.

Part B: Each expansion cohort (B1, B2, B3) targets approximately 40
patients. Based on published data for PD-1 inhibitors in dMMR/MSI-H mCRC
(e.g., pembrolizumab: ORR \~33% in ≥2L; \~45% in 1L), assuming a target
ORR of ≥35% (≥2L checkpoint-naive, B1), with a null hypothesis ORR of
≤15%, and using a one-stage Flemings design (α = 0.05, 1-β = 0.80), a
sample size of 40 evaluable patients per cohort is required. This
provides ≥80% power to detect a clinically meaningful ORR.

**5.8.2 Analysis Populations**

- Safety Population: All patients who receive any dose of Cervimab.

- DLT Evaluable Population (Part A): All patients completing the DLT
  evaluation window without a protocol violation affecting DLT
  assessment.

- Efficacy Evaluable Population (Part B): All patients receiving ≥1 dose
  of Cervimab at RP2D and having ≥1 post-baseline tumor assessment.

- PK Analysis Population: All patients with ≥1 measurable post-dose PK
  concentration.

- Full Analysis Set (FAS): All enrolled and dosed patients.

**5.8.3 Primary Efficacy Analysis**

ORR (Part B) will be estimated with 95% exact binomial confidence
intervals (Clopper-Pearson method). Response data will be based on BICR
assessment per RECIST 1.1. Secondary endpoints will be estimated using
Kaplan-Meier methodology (PFS, DOR, OS). Exploratory biomarker analyses
will use logistic regression, Cox proportional hazards, and Spearman
correlation as appropriate.
