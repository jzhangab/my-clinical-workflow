**1. Introduction and ICH S9 Context**

Per ICH S9 (Nonclinical Evaluation for Anticancer Pharmaceuticals,
November 2009), routine genotoxicity testing is NOT required for
biopharmaceuticals (monoclonal antibodies) intended for the treatment of
patients with advanced cancer. This position is grounded in the
following scientific rationale:

- Monoclonal antibodies are large protein molecules (\~144 kDa) that do
  not enter the nucleus and are not metabolically converted to reactive
  electrophilic species capable of direct DNA interaction.

- Cervimab is a fully human IgG4 monoclonal antibody with no reactive
  functional groups, no intercalating moieties, and no known
  small-molecule genotoxic impurities at ICH Q3A/Q3B threshold levels.

- The mechanism of action (PD-1 receptor blockade) does not involve
  DNA-reactive metabolites or oxidative DNA damage.

- ICH S6(R1) explicitly states that genotoxicity testing is not
  applicable to proteins and peptides.

Despite the regulatory waiver, Meridian Oncology Sciences conducted a
limited genotoxicity assessment as due diligence, focusing on
mutagenicity of the formulation excipients and drug substance-related
impurities (not the protein itself, per ICH S6R1).

**5. Conclusion and Regulatory Statement**

Based on:

- Regulatory waiver applicable under ICH S9 and ICH S6(R1) for
  monoclonal antibody genotoxicity testing,

- Absence of mechanistic basis for protein-mediated DNA interaction,

- No genotoxic structural alerts in Cervimab peptide fragments (in
  silico),

- All drug substance and formulation impurities below ICH Q3A/Q3C/Q3D
  and TTC thresholds,

- No known or predicted genotoxic impurities (NGIs) identified by in
  silico screening,

**Genotoxicity Testing: NOT REQUIRED. No safety concern identified.
Standard Ames test, chromosomal aberration assay, and in vivo
micronucleus test are NOT conducted and are not required to support
Phase 1 clinical trials or marketing authorization per applicable ICH
guidelines.**
