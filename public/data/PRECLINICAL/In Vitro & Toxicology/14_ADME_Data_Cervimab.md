**1. Introduction: mAb ADME Principles**

Cervimab is a large molecular weight protein therapeutic (\~144 kDa)
subject to the universal ADME principles governing IgG4 monoclonal
antibodies. These fundamentally differ from small-molecule drugs:

- Absorption: No meaningful oral or inhaled bioavailability due to GI
  proteolysis and mucosal impermeability. IV infusion (60 min) is the
  route of administration. Distribution is restricted primarily to the
  central (vascular) compartment with limited tissue penetration (Vss
  \~60 mL/kg ≈ plasma + interstitial fluid volume of the vascular
  system).

- Distribution: Governed by FcRn-mediated transcytosis across
  endothelial cells (major route to interstitial space) and non-specific
  fluid-phase endocytosis. Target organ distribution (tumor, lymphoid
  tissue) occurs via convection/transcytosis.

- Metabolism: Cervimab is catabolized by lysosomal proteases (Cathepsins
  D, B, L, etc.) to its constituent amino acids and small peptides
  across all cells capable of pinocytosis, primarily in vascular
  endothelium, liver Kupffer cells, and reticuloendothelial system
  (RES). The catabolites are recycled as endogenous amino acids. NO
  cytochrome P450-mediated metabolism. No reactive metabolites. No
  small-molecule metabolite safety assessment required.

- Excretion: Intact mAb is NOT renally filtered (MW 144 kDa \>\>
  glomerular filtration cutoff \~50 kDa). Catabolized amino acids are
  excreted normally through urine and feces as endogenous metabolites.
  No parent drug appears in urine or feces.

**3.2 Blood:Plasma Partitioning**

Blood-to-plasma ratio: 0.62 ± 0.04 (cynomolgus monkey; n=6;
concentration range 1--100 µg/mL; measured by liquid scintillation
counting of \[¹²⁵I\]-cervimab). This ratio is consistent with
theoretical expectation for a large IgG4 molecule (excluded from
erythrocytes; predominantly plasma-distributed). No
concentration-dependence in blood:plasma ratio. Confirms use of plasma
as the bioanalytical matrix for all PK analyses.

**4. Metabolism**

**4.1 Catabolic Pathway --- Proteolytic Degradation**

As a protein therapeutic, Cervimab is exclusively eliminated via
proteolytic catabolism. No cytochrome P450 metabolites, glucuronides,
sulfates, or other typical small-molecule phase I/II metabolites are
formed or expected. Cervimab catabolism proceeds via the following
pathway:

- Internalization: Pinocytosis of Cervimab--PD-1 complex
  (target-mediated) or non-specific fluid-phase endocytosis
  (FcRn-independent) into endosomes of vascular endothelial cells,
  Kupffer cells, monocyte-derived macrophages.

- Endosomal sorting: At pH 5.5--6.0 in early endosomes, FcRn binds
  Cervimab Fc (IgG4 Fc with S228P stabilization) with high affinity,
  diverting the antibody to recycling endosomes and back to the cell
  surface (pH 7.4 → FcRn dissociation → Cervimab released to
  circulation). This recycling pathway extends t½ from \~12h (without
  FcRn) to \~20 days.

- Lysosomal degradation: Cervimab not rescued by FcRn is trafficked to
  lysosomes where Cathepsin D, B, L cleave the polypeptide backbone at
  non-specific sites. Final catabolites: individual amino acids and
  small dipeptides (2--6 aa), which re-enter amino acid pools.

- No reactive intermediates: Unlike CYP-metabolized small molecules,
  protein catabolism does not generate electrophilic species. Cervimab
  Fc glycans (complex N-glycans at Asn297) are cleaved by lysosomal
  glycosidases to monosaccharides (GlcNAc, mannose, fucose, galactose,
  sialic acid) --- all endogenous metabolites.
